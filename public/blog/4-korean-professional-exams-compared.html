The Four Major Korean Professional Exams Compared (2026)

2026 · ~1,800 words · 9 min read

The Korean professional-exam market is more crowded than any single brochure makes it look. Search engines flood you with promotional content from prep academies and recruitment agencies whose incentives are exactly aligned with telling you that their exam is the best fit. This article does the opposite: it puts the four most widely chosen professional credentials side by side and shows you, on each axis that actually matters, where they really stand.

The four credentials in scope:

  • PMP — Project Management Professional, administered by PMI (USA)
  • CPLA (공인노무사) — Certified Public Labor Attorney, administered by HRD Korea
  • Industrial Safety Consultant (산업안전지도사) — administered by HRD Korea
  • Industrial Health Consultant (산업보건지도사) — administered by HRD Korea

Eligibility: who can actually sit?

The biggest practical filter is at the very front. PMP is the only credential on this list that requires demonstrable prior work experience: 36 months of project work plus 35 contact hours of formal PM education for degree-holders, 60 months for non-degree-holders. Those numbers are checked through a written application before you ever schedule the exam, and PMI audits roughly 1 in 50 applications.

The three Korean credentials are nominally open to anyone aged 20 or older. In practice the open-eligibility doesn't mean what it sounds like — the pass-rate math (overall ~3% across all stages) means the entry filter happens during the exam itself, not before.

Exam architecture and where you actually fail

The PMP is the simplest format on this list — one sitting, 180 questions, 230 minutes, mostly multiple choice with some multiple-response and a handful of other types. You either pass or you don't, and a retake is straightforward.

The three Korean consultant/lawyer exams all follow a multi-stage gauntlet. Stage 1 is objective multiple-choice, similar in feel to a typical certification exam. Stage 2 is the cliff: a written essay where you have to produce 4–6 handwritten pages of structured legal or technical analysis per question, in Korean, under time pressure. Stage 2 is where 85–93% of Stage-1 passers wash out.

Stage 3, where it exists (Safety and Health Consultant), is an in-person interview. Pass rates here are high (~88–90%) because the panel mostly catches candidates who clearly memorized without understanding. If you reached Stage 3 with real working knowledge, you almost always pass.

Prep time and cost

PMP at 2–3 months for a working professional is the outlier. The Korean three-stage exams are all 1- to 2-year commitments, and most successful candidates report studying meaningfully on evenings and weekends for the full duration. Expecting to pass any of the three in under 12 months as a part-time studier is unrealistic for almost everyone.

The cost picture is similarly bimodal. PMP's USD 405 exam fee plus an optional 0–2M KRW for prep materials is one tier. The Korean exams are nominally cheap (under KRW 200k per stage) but the prep-academy market around them runs KRW 3–15 million for a full study cycle. Those numbers are not a cartel — they reflect the genuine difficulty of teaching essay-writing under time pressure and walking candidates through dozens of practice fact patterns with feedback.

Career value

This is where the four credentials diverge most sharply.

PMP is a portable, internationally recognized credential. It signals "I have read the standard project-management body of knowledge and can pass an exam on it" — which inside a corporate hiring funnel translates into resume-screen advantages, occasional pay-grade differentials (often KRW 200k–500k per month in Korean roles), and visibility for project-lead positions. It is rarely a license to do anything specific; it is a credential you trade for opportunities.

The three Korean consultant/lawyer credentials are licenses in the strict sense. License-holders are legally authorized to perform regulated work — representing clients in labor-administrative proceedings (CPLA), signing off on workplace-safety management plans (Safety Consultant), or being retained as a workplace's required external health advisor (Health Consultant). That regulated scope produces a more concentrated income distribution: license-holders who build a practice typically earn KRW 80–200 million per year, with a long tail of solo practitioners both above and below that range.

Which one should you pick?

The right answer depends on three things: your current career stage, your time horizon, and your tolerance for multi-year preparation cycles.

  • If you have 3+ years of project experience and want a quick credential boost: PMP. Best ROI for the time invested.
  • If you want to leave salaried employment and run your own practice: CPLA is the most established route, with a clear demand pipeline in labor disputes and a regulated competitive moat.
  • If you have an engineering background and want to specialize in regulated workplace safety: Safety Consultant. The demand side keeps growing as more Korean firms outsource safety compliance.
  • If you have a public-health, nursing, or industrial-hygiene background: Health Consultant is the natural professional extension.
