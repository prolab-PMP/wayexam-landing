CPLA (공인노무사) Eligibility, Process, and Career Path (2026)

2026 · ~1,700 words · 9 min read

The 공인노무사 license (CPLA — Certified Public Labor Attorney) is one of the three "national professional licenses" recognized in Korean labor practice, alongside attorney (변호사) and certified tax accountant (세무사). The English name "labor attorney" understates what license-holders actually do: while they can represent clients in labor-related administrative proceedings, they do not practice litigation in civil courts. Their core regulated work is closer to a hybrid of HR consultant, labor-relations advisor, and administrative-court advocate.

This guide walks through the eligibility, the two-stage exam, the typical 18–24 month prep timeline, and what license-holders do for a living once they pass.

Who runs the exam

The CPLA exam is administered by HRD Korea (한국산업인력공단) under the Ministry of Employment and Labor. The legal basis is the Certified Public Labor Attorney Act (공인노무사법). Annual exam scheduling, registration, fee collection, and result publication all happen through HRD Korea's portal at q-net.or.kr.

Eligibility

The headline eligibility is simple: anyone aged 20 or older with no criminal disqualification can apply. There is no education requirement, no work-experience requirement, and no nationality requirement. In that sense the CPLA exam is genuinely open.

That openness is partly an illusion produced by the exam itself. The Stage-1 pass rate hovers around 28% and the Stage-2 pass rate is roughly 9% of Stage-1 passers, so the actual proportion of registrants who walk away with a license in any single year is closer to 2–3%. The exam is the filter, not the application form.

The two-stage exam

Stage 1 — objective

Stage 1 is a written objective exam with five subjects:

  • Labor Law I (노동법I) — Labor Standards Act and adjacent statutes
  • Labor Law II (노동법II) — labor union / industrial-relations law
  • Civil Law (민법) — general principles and contract
  • Social Insurance Law (사회보험법) — pension, health, employment, industrial-accident insurance
  • Economics or Business Administration (경제학원론 / 경영학개론) — choose one

200 questions across the five subjects, 200 minutes total. You must average 60% across all subjects and score at least 40% on every individual subject. The 40%-floor rule is what catches most marginal candidates — they pass the overall average but flunk one subject below the floor.

Stage 2 — written essay

Stage 2 is a handwritten essay exam with three subjects: Labor Law I, Labor Law II, and HR Management (인사노무관리론), plus one elective from a short list. Each subject presents 2–3 fact patterns and asks for structured legal analysis: identify the issue, apply the relevant statute and case law, reach a conclusion. Per essay, candidates typically produce 4–6 handwritten pages.

Stage 2 is the bottleneck. Roughly 90% of Stage-1 passers fail Stage 2 on their first attempt. The reason is not subject-matter ignorance — most Stage-1 passers know the underlying law — but inability to produce structured legal writing under time pressure. Practicing essays without feedback is almost useless; this is why most successful candidates work with a private tutor or academy through Stage 2.

Carry-over

Stage 1 passers who fail Stage 2 retain their Stage 1 pass for two more sittings, meaning Stage 2 can be attempted up to three times without retaking Stage 1. Many license-holders earn the credential on their second or third Stage-2 attempt.

Typical preparation timeline

An honest baseline is 18–24 months of part-time-equivalent study. A representative timeline for a working professional studying evenings and weekends:

  • Months 1–6 — foundation. Read the major textbooks for each Stage-1 subject. Don't drill questions yet; build the conceptual scaffolding first. This phase commonly takes longer than people expect because labor law and civil law are dense.
  • Months 7–12 — Stage-1 question phase. Switch to past-question drilling. Target 50–80 questions per day, with a structured error log that captures the specific statute, case, or concept missed.
  • Months 13–18 — Stage-2 writing phase. Begin structured essay practice with mentor feedback. Aim to produce 2–3 full essays per week and review each within two days while the writing is still fresh.
  • Months 19–24 — integration. Mock-exam-heavy phase. Take full-length practice exams under time pressure at least twice a month for Stage 1, and full-length essay sets for Stage 2.

Cost

Registration fees themselves are modest: roughly KRW 30,000 for Stage 1 and KRW 50,000 for Stage 2. The real cost is preparation. A typical 18-month CPLA prep cycle costs between KRW 5 million and KRW 15 million depending on academy choice, intensity, and whether you take in-person live classes versus recorded video.

What license-holders actually do

The CPLA license authorizes its holder to act as legal representative in five regulated categories:

  • Filing and arguing labor-related administrative cases before the Labor Relations Commission and the Ministry of Employment and Labor
  • Representing employers or workers in unfair-dismissal and unfair-labor-practice disputes
  • Drafting and reviewing collective-bargaining agreements and rules of employment
  • Filing and managing social-insurance claims and disputes
  • Conducting workplace investigations into harassment, wage-and-hour violations, and similar issues

The largest single income source for most license-holders is corporate retainer work — being on call for an employer to handle the labor-administrative items above on a monthly fixed fee. Solo practitioners commonly carry 10–30 retainer clients across SMEs and earn KRW 80–200 million annually before tax. License-holders at large law firms operating labor-practice teams earn meaningfully more but trade autonomy for it.

If you're deciding whether to start

The honest test is whether you can commit to 18+ months of regular study. The exam mathematically rewards persistence — the cohort that passes is mostly the cohort that didn't give up after the first Stage-2 failure. If you genuinely cannot promise yourself 10–15 study hours per week sustained for a year and a half, the realistic answer is that this credential is not currently the right fit, even though the upside if you finish is real.
