A 6-Month Study Plan for the Industrial Safety Consultant Exam (Stage 1)

2026 · ~1,500 words · 8 min read

This article lays out a six-month, week-by-week plan for the Stage-1 portion of the Industrial Safety Consultant exam (산업안전지도사 1차). It's written for working safety engineers — typically people who already hold the Industrial Safety Engineer license (산업안전기사) and are looking to step up. Stage 2 (essay) and Stage 3 (interview) need their own dedicated cycles and are not covered here.

Assumptions

  • You can commit 10–12 study hours per week consistently.
  • You have working-level familiarity with industrial-safety vocabulary already.
  • Target sitting: the next available Stage-1 administration roughly 6 months out.

The Stage-1 subject map

Stage 1 tests common subjects plus one specialty:

  • Common subjects: industrial-safety law (산업안전관계법령), industrial hygiene (산업위생일반), ergonomics (인간공학), system safety (시스템안전공학)
  • Specialty (pick one): machine safety (기계안전), electrical safety (전기안전), chemical safety (화공안전), construction safety (건설안전)

The six-month plan below is organized to give the common subjects their due before drilling deep on the specialty. If you swap the specialty, the rest of the plan stays roughly the same.

Checkpoint at month 2: you should have a 25–40 page personal outline covering every Stage-1 subject. The outline is your reference document; it is not memorization material. Don't skip this step — candidates who go straight to question-drilling without an outline tend to plateau around month 4.

Months 3–4: question drill

Switch to past-question drill. Use a 3-year archive of past Stage-1 papers as the spine of the work. Each week:

  • Drill one subject's worth of past questions (≈100 questions)
  • Maintain an error log: question number, subject, what you missed, the specific paragraph in your outline that covers it
  • Review your error log on Saturday — re-do every question you got wrong in the prior 4 weeks

Checkpoint at month 4: on a fresh past-paper, you should average 65% overall with no subject below 50%. If any subject is below 50%, the next two weeks of month 5 should add an extra drill block for that subject before moving forward.

Month 5: weakness elimination

Stop reading the outline as primary material. Spend month 5 on three things only:

  • Re-drilling your error log — every question you missed in months 3–4
  • Targeted reading on the 2–3 subtopics where your error log clusters
  • One full-length mock paper per week under time pressure (150 minutes, all 100 questions)

Checkpoint at month 5: full-length mock results should be above 65% on three consecutive papers. If they're not, you're not ready and one more month of error-log elimination is worth more than rushing to the real exam.

Month 6: peaking

The final month is about familiarity and pacing, not new learning. Each week:

  • One full-length timed mock
  • One subject-focused 100-question drill on the subject where your variance is highest
  • A 30-minute "key formulas and statute numbers" review on Sundays — the small stuff you'd otherwise forget under exam pressure

The Saturday before the exam: light review only. The day of the exam: arrive early, hydrate, and use the first 10 minutes of the test to skim the whole paper to identify quick-win questions you can lock in before tackling harder ones.

Common failure modes

  • Skipping the outline phase and going straight to question-drilling. Without the outline you accumulate trivia rather than understanding.
  • Drilling without an error log. You repeat the same mistakes for months and don't notice.
  • Studying only your specialty. Specialty drops to 80%+ accuracy while common subjects stagnate. Stage 1 has a per-subject 40% floor — strong specialty doesn't save you from a weak common subject.
  • Saving mock exams for the last month. Time-pressure stamina is a separate skill from subject knowledge; train it earlier.
