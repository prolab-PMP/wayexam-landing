PMP — Project Management Professional

Exam guide · Updated 2026

What it is

PMP (Project Management Professional) is a certification administered by the Project Management Institute (PMI), a U.S.-based non-profit. It is the most widely recognized project-management credential in the world, with more than one million certified professionals across roughly 200 countries. Unlike most Korean licenses, PMP is not tied to a specific country's labor law — it tests a general body of project-management knowledge that applies across industries.

Eligibility

To sit for the exam you need both project-management experience and formal training:

  • If you have a four-year degree: 36 months of project-management experience within the past eight years, plus 35 contact hours of project-management education.
  • If you have a high-school diploma or associate degree: 60 months of project-management experience within the past eight years, plus the same 35 contact hours.

"Project-management experience" does not require the word "manager" in your job title. PMI accepts experience as a coordinator, team lead, or contributor as long as you can describe the project initiating, planning, executing, monitoring, and closing work you led or supported.

Exam format

The 2026 exam is computer-based with 180 questions to be answered in 230 minutes (two optional ten-minute breaks). Question types include multiple choice (one correct answer), multiple response (two or more correct answers, partial credit usually not granted), matching, hotspot, and limited fill-in-the-blank. Roughly half of all questions are scenario-based — you read a short paragraph describing a project situation and pick the best next action.

Content domains

The current Examination Content Outline (ECO) splits content into three domains:

  • People (42%) — leadership, team building, conflict resolution, stakeholder engagement, mentoring.
  • Process (50%) — scope, schedule, cost, quality, risk, procurement, and integration management; methodology selection (Agile vs Hybrid vs Predictive).
  • Business Environment (8%) — compliance, value delivery, organizational change, external factors.

Approximately half of the questions are written with an Agile or Hybrid context and the other half are Predictive. The 2026 ECO transition aligns the exam with PMBOK 8, which sharpens the principles-and-performance-domains framing introduced in PMBOK 7.

Pass rate & difficulty

PMI does not publish an official pass rate, but practitioner surveys consistently put first-attempt pass rates in the 60–70% range. The exam is challenging less because the underlying material is conceptually hard and more because it requires you to translate textbook concepts into situational judgment — knowing what a Risk Register is gets you maybe 10% of the way; knowing what to do next when a sponsor changes scope two weeks before a release is what the exam actually tests.

Recommended preparation

A reasonable timeline for someone with relevant work experience is 8–12 weeks of focused study:

  • Weeks 1–3: read a current PMP prep book end to end. Don't try to memorize — read for the mental model.
  • Weeks 4–6: drill questions by domain, starting with People and Process. Aim for 30–50 questions a day.
  • Weeks 7–9: take full-length timed mock exams. Target 70%+ on three consecutive mocks before scheduling the real exam.
  • Weeks 10–12: review only the question categories where your accuracy is below 75%, plus all wrong answers from your mocks.

Cost (2026)

The exam fee is USD 405 for non-members of PMI and USD 284 for PMI members. PMI membership itself is USD 159 per year plus a USD 10 application fee. If you plan to take the exam and want even one retake or to use the PMBOK Guide that members get for free, the math usually favors becoming a member.
